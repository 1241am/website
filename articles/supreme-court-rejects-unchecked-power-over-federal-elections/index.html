Supreme Court Rejects Unchecked Power Over Federal Elections

June 27, 2023

The Supreme Court on Tuesday rejected a legal theory that would have given state legislatures largely unchecked power to set rules for federal elections and draw congressional maps. The 6-to-3 majority, led by Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr., dismissed the theory as it would have violated the ordinary constraints imposed by state law. The ruling was a victory for those who opposed the theory, which would have removed safeguards against partisan gerrymandering and curtailed the ability to challenge voting restrictions in state courts. It also gave federal courts the power to second-guess some rulings of state courts based on state law.

  1. The Supreme Court rejected a legal theory that would have given state legislatures largely unchecked power over federal elections.
  2. The vote was 6 to 3, with Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr. writing the majority opinion.
  3. The case concerned the “independent state legislature” theory, which is based on a reading of the Constitution’s Elections Clause.
  4. The ruling soundly dismissed the theory, which was viewed as extreme and dangerous by lawyers, judges, and scholars across the ideological spectrum.
  5. The decision elevated the power of federal courts in the process, allowing them to second-guess at least some rulings of state courts based on state law.

"It was as if a giant wall was built to protect the federal elections, with Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr. holding the cement mixer. The extreme and dangerous "independent state legislature" theory was soundly rejected, allowing federal courts to keep a watchful eye over state law decisions."
